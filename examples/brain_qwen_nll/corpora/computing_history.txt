The history of computing began with the invention of the abacus in ancient Mesopotamia. Mechanical calculators followed in the seventeenth century, with Blaise Pascal building the Pascaline in 1642. Charles Babbage designed the Analytical Engine in the 1830s, although it was never completed in his lifetime. Ada Lovelace wrote what is considered the first computer program for that machine. The twentieth century brought electronic computers: ENIAC was completed in 1945 at the University of Pennsylvania. Alan Turing formalized the theoretical foundation with his work on computability and the Turing machine. Claude Shannon connected boolean algebra to circuit design, enabling reliable digital logic. Transistors replaced vacuum tubes in the 1950s, and integrated circuits arrived in the 1960s. The personal computer revolution started in the 1970s with machines like the Altair and the Apple II. Networking grew through ARPANET in the same decade, eventually becoming the modern internet. The World Wide Web was invented by Tim Berners-Lee at CERN in 1989. Mobile computing took over in the 2000s, and machine learning rose to prominence in the 2010s with deep neural networks.
